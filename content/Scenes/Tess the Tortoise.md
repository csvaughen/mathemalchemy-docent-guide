# Tess the Tortoise

**Math:** [[Zeno's Paradoxes and Limits]] · [[Infinite Series and Convergence]] · [[Measure Theory and the Lebesgue Integral]] (at Integral Hill)

## What it is
Tess is the exhibit's beloved mascot — a knitted tortoise with a ceramic shell — journeying along **Zeno's Path**, a quilted trail whose paver segments shrink as she approaches her destination, embodying Zeno's paradox of motion. The **Riemann Wall** runs alongside the path, carrying a **Hilbert's Hotel** billboard (the paradox of the infinite hotel that's always "full" yet always has room for one more guest). The path leads to **Integral Hill**, which stages two rival ways of measuring the same shape side by side: **Lebesgue Terraces** — wooden platforms shaped as genuine level curves of a two-variable function, so climbing them is literally slicing the hill horizontally — and, visible from the top, the **Riemann Cliffs** — 300-plus hexagonal dowels cut to varied heights forming a "noisy" height graph, the vertical-rectangle approach from Calc I. (Distinct from the unrelated café scene also called a "terrace" — see [[The Terrace]].)

## Docent questions
- "If Tess always has to travel half the remaining distance, will she ever truly arrive — or walk forever?"
- "Two structures on this hill both measure the same shape — one in horizontal slices, one in vertical columns. Which is the terraces, and which is the cliffs?"
- "Hilbert's Hotel is always full, but there's always room for one more guest — how?"

## Key vocabulary
- **Zeno's Paradox** — motion seems impossible because reaching a goal requires infinitely many halfway steps.
- **Level curve** — the set of points where a function of two variables takes the same value; each Lebesgue Terrace is one.
- **Lebesgue integral** — measuring by horizontal slices (function value), vs. the Riemann integral's vertical slices (domain) — staged here as terraces vs. cliffs.
- **Hilbert's Hotel** — a hotel with infinitely many rooms, always full, that can still make room for infinitely many new guests — a hands-on paradox of countable infinity.

## Activity: "Asymptotic Approach" (Zeno's Path)
Pick a line on the floor; the group stands a few feet back. Repeat the instruction: *"Take a step covering exactly half the remaining distance."* Steps become tiny shuffles — the group converges on the line without crossing it. The **limit** of the journey is exactly that line.

**Takeaway:** infinity can be tamed by a limit — infinitely many small steps can still lead to a definite end.

## Activity: "Rising Tide Measurement" (Integral Hill)
Everyone stands in an imaginary dry pool. Call rising "water levels" — knees! waist! shoulders! — freezing everyone below each line. You've measured the group layer-by-layer (horizontal, Lebesgue-style, like climbing the terraces) rather than person-by-person (vertical, Riemann-style, like the cliffs' dowels).

**Takeaway:** slicing the world horizontally is often the smartest way to measure complexity — and this hill stages both methods side by side so you can compare them directly.
