# Activity: Cipher Safari

The [[Cryptography Quilt]] is 20 labeled blocks (A–T), arranged clockwise in four tiers of rising complexity, and every single one depicts a real cryptographic idea — not decoration. This activity is as simple as it gets: look at a block, guess what code or cipher it's showing, then check. No materials, no setup, nothing to touch. It's built to work in the first five minutes anyone spends in the room, which makes it a natural opener for a scout troop or a class walking in behind a teacher.

**5–20 minutes, scales with how many blocks you cover. Any group size.**

## The hook 
> "Before I say a word — look at this quilt. Twenty panels, and every one of them is hiding a real code or cipher, from something Julius Caesar actually used to math that's still being invented today. Pick a block. What do you think it's showing?"

## How to play
- **Quick version (arriving/gallery pass-through):** point to three or four blocks spanning different tiers, let the group guess out loud, then reveal. Two minutes gets a laugh and a hook; five minutes gets real engagement.
- **Full sweep (classroom or a longer visit):** split into small teams, hand each team a tier (five blocks), and work clockwise from Tier 1 to Tier 4 — basic symbols first, modern mathematical cryptography last. Each team reports back what they think their blocks show before anyone checks the answers below.

## The four tiers, block by block

### Tier 1 — Basic & universally recognized symbols
- **E — Electricity plug.** Modern cryptography runs on computers, and computers run on electricity — the most basic dependency in the whole quilt.
- **I — DNA double helix.** DNA is the code of life, and it has its own knot problem: it has to be tightly wound to fit inside a cell, and then carefully un-knotted to be read → [[Knotical (Knotilus Bay)]].
- **Q — Shield.** The plainest symbol here: cryptography's whole job is protection — credit card numbers, passwords, anything worth guarding.
- **S — Knitting Morse code.** Two knitted figures "converse" in Morse code worked into cables and colorwork, echoing real history — women hid messages in their knitting during both World Wars.
- **T — Fingerprint.** A code unique to you, historically used to verify identity by hand and now the basis of biometric security.

### Tier 2 — Historical cryptography
- **F — Carrier pigeon & letter locking.** Pigeons physically carried messages; "letter locking" is the intricate 17th–18th-century art of folding and sealing a letter so any tampering would show.
- **J — Caesar cipher.** Julius Caesar's own trick, per the historian Suetonius: shift every letter of the alphabet the same fixed number of places — his shift was 3.
- **N — Pigpen cipher.** Letters are hidden as shapes: draw a tic-tac-toe grid and an X grid over the alphabet, and each letter becomes the outline of the "pen" it sits in.
- **P — Quipu.** The Inca system of knotted, colored strings used to record numbers — a genuine knot-based data code, centuries before anyone drew a quilt block about it.
- **R — Scytale.** Wrap a strip of paper around a stick and write straight across it; unwrap the strip and the letters scramble. The stick's diameter is the key — one of the oldest ciphers on record.

### Tier 3 — Complex cryptography
- **A — Hamming code (sending).** The encoding half of a genuine error-correcting code: extra digits get added to a message so that an error picked up in transmission can be caught and fixed on the other end.
- **C — Vigenère cipher.** A whole table of alphabet shifts instead of just Caesar's one, catalogued by Blaise de Vigenère in 1585 — considered unbreakable for centuries.
- **G — Knapsack cryptography.** Built on the "knapsack problem": given only a total weight, which items (weights) were combined to make it? Easy to check, brutally hard to reverse without the key.
- **K — Hamming code (receiving).** The other half of Block A — the receiving end recovers the original, correct message from one that may have picked up an error.
- **M — Enigma machine.** The WWII rotor cipher machine, used in different configurations by multiple sides — every keystroke shifts the internal wiring before the next letter is even chosen.

### Tier 4 — Modern mathematical cryptography
- **B — Blockchain.** A stylized take on the math structure underneath cryptocurrencies like Bitcoin.
- **D — Elliptic curve cryptography.** Curves shaped like y² = x³ + ax + b, where "adding" two points on the curve produces a third — the operation underneath a huge amount of real-world encryption today.
- **H — Lattice-based cryptography.** Security built on the "shortest vector problem" in a grid of points: trivial with a "good" secret basis, brutally hard with only a "bad" public one. One of the leading candidates for cryptography that can survive quantum computers.
- **L — Pollard's rho algorithm.** A method for factoring numbers that are the product of two primes — named for the Greek letter ρ because the iterative process it uses eventually loops into a cycle, like the shape of the letter itself.
- **O — ℤ/pℤ is cyclic.** Modular arithmetic where repeatedly multiplying a number cycles through every other number, mod a prime — the engine inside Diffie–Hellman key exchange.

## Why this activity earns its spot
It costs nothing to run, needs no setup, and turns a wall of quilted squares into twenty separate hooks — pick whichever one lands with your group (WWII history for one crowd, DNA for a bio class, blockchain for teenagers) and let that be the door in. It also front-loads vocabulary — cipher, key, error-correction — that pays off immediately once the same group tries [[Decode the Padlock]] or [[Decode the Ring]] at the center of the same quilt.

## Materials
None. Just the quilt itself.
